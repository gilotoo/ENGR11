Design 3D Printing Project

Prusa Mini Assembly

We began our project by unboxing our Prusa Mini package and separated all the parts (mise en place). Following the manual, we were able to assemble the printer by following step by step instructions provided in the manual. The assembly included combining the y and z axis of the printer, assembling the x-axis and extruder, assembling the print head and heatbed and finally, assembling the LCD and electronics. Once the printer was fully assembled, we then loaded it with a filament color of our choosing.

Drawbacks after Assembly

While constructing our printer, we ran into just a single issue once the assembly was almost complete. When loading the filament toward the end of the assembly, the filament was not able to fully enter through the tube in the printer. This issue was eventually fixed by having to loosen the filament holder and reinsert the filament tube connection. This method eventually solved the problem and the printer was able to operate normally!

Reflections after Assembly

After completely constructing the Prusa Mini Printer, we were left with a couple reflections for improvements. We concluded that the manual could have included more detailed images of the steps of the building process. Overall, we found the assembly process to be straightforward but challenging in the best way. Our group enjoyed the fact that a majority of the assembly was just placing built pieces together and not having to fully construct the printer from scratch.

Prusa Mini Calibration

Our next step of the process was to calibrate the printer so we could print our first model. The calibration process was more simple than the assembly process. For calibration, my partner (Daniel) and I had to go to the 'calibration' option on the printer once we turned it on. After we selected that option, the printer began a process of placing continuous lines of filament in a maze pattern on the heatbed. Or task during calibration was to change the z-axis height of the nozzle extruding the filament so the extrusion would stick to the heatbed just right.

Drawbacks after Callibration

After calibrating the printer the first time, we noticed the filament was not adhering to the heatbed well enough for a 3-D modeled print. We fixed this by adjusting the z-axis height by a significant amount during the second calibration.

Reflections after Callibration

After calibrating the printer, we concluded that calibration was a fairly simple process. Once we understood that the dial on the printer moves the nozzle in very small increments, we were less lenient with the amount of turns needed to change the height of the nozzle. Now that we have the printer calibrated, it was time to print our first design!

3D Benchy Model Print

Now that we had our printer built and calibrated it was time to print. Our first print was already downloaded onto the hard drive provided in the assembly. The print design was called "3D Benchy '' and it was a design of a mini tugboat with many different dimensions included. We retrieved the file for the model from the Prusa website and placed the model in our CAD program (Prusa Slicer). We took a look at the different specs for the print. Once we were satisfied, we exported the file onto the hard drive. We plugged the hard drive in and let the printer run the print. At the end, the print ran smoothly and came out with the exact model we were hoping for.

Speeding up the Process

After completing a successful build, we wanted to decrease the print time of our model by tinkering the settings in our Prusa Slicer program. We exported the same file onto the Prusa Slicer software and made changes to the specs of the model. We increased the layer height and first layer height, decreased the amount of solid layers, decreased shell thickness, changed seam position, decreased infill density, and changed the infill pattern. Overall, this decreased the time to print from an hour to 42 minutes.

Reflections after First Prints

Overall the print process went very smoothly. We were able to complete these prints without running into any printer malfunctions or software issues. Comparing the first and second print, you can see physically where the changes are in the print models in comparison to one another. Having to put the file onto Prusa Slicer is also a straightforward process and the software makes it easy to follow.

Combining Multiple Designs

The objective of this project was to use our understanding of additive and subtractive manufacturing to create a design model using multiple designs off a public space. We were tasked to combine designs to make the new design better than the original.

Design Search and Sketch

The first step of the design process was to find designs on a public website that I could use to create a new one. I browsed through the website Thingaverse and saw some cool designs I liked. I decided to use the design of a bottle sleeve for cans and bottles. I came up with the idea to add another design of a bottle opener to add to the bottle sleeve for convenience. I sketched out my idea and when I found designs that would fit my idea, I exported them to the CAD software TinkerCad.

First Design

Once I got my two designs (bottle sleeve and opener) on tinkercad, I began combining the designs together. I isolated the opener section of the bottle opener by removing the handle of the bottle opener. Next, I placed the opener on the bottle sleeve in the same orientation I had in my design sketch. Once I liked the design I created, I exported it to Prusa Slicer software to be printed. I kept the default printer settings on Prusa and generated support material to help the print.

Problems with First Design

I ran into a couple problems with the design of my first prototype. First, the diimensions of the interior opening of the bottle sleeve did not fit the sizing of a regular glass bottle. The design of the original sleeve had an oblique opening that I did not see originally. Secondly, the height of the sleeve was too short and that caused stability issues when a bottle was inside the sleeve. Third, the opener was not sturdy enough to use for a bottle top and it was not there was a lack of comfortability when using the sleeve because of the opener's posiiton.

Second Design

I went back to the drawing board and fixed the issues that the first design had. I fixed the opening of the sleeve to fit the regular size glass bottle by making the diameter 2.5 inches. I increased the height of the bottle sleeve from three to six inches. I also took the bottle opener portion and flushed it to the sleeve, making it engraved in the bottle rather than sticking out awkwardly.

Final Result

Once I was comfortbale with my changes, I exported the file to Prusa Slicer and kept the default printer settings. The only thing I changed in the Prusa software was the generated support. I sliced the print and placed it on a Prusa Mini+ printer. The print came out very successful and there was no problem with my process.

Reflection

Overall, this project was a very open-ended one that had a lot of steps along the way. The first design of my print came out successful and served some purpose but could have been better. The second design on CAD was an improvement but struggled in its transfer to the Prusa software and physical print. In the future, I would optimize my design by including a handle for the bottle sleeve so the user can have a better grip on it. I would also add insulation material to the inside of the sleeve to keep the drink of choice at a cold temperature.

Alarm Clock Design

The objective of this assignment was to design and construct a functioning alarm clock. I have detailed the process by which this was accomplished below.

Soldering Circuit Board

The first part of the project was to solder our alarm clock circuit kit together. After an informational demonstration in class on how to solder, we as a team soldered elements of a circuit onto the board according to the diagram given. We soldered elements such as resisitors, displays, and buttons. While soldering, we came across difficult elements to solder. Some of the elements were very close in proximity and that caused the metal to combine in orientations that were undesirable. To fix this issue, we extracted the metal using a suction tool and reapplied the soldering metal in the desirable orientation. Once we completed the soldering of all the elements, the next part was to confirm that our circuit operated and ran properly. We snipped the extended ends of wire on the back of the circuit and connected a battery to the circuit board. Fortunately, the circuit lit up and was functioning properly.

Alarm Clock Circuit Measurements

Once we had the circuit board constructed, it was time to design our alarm clock surroundings. The first step we conducted was getting a drawing of the layout of our alarm clock. We wanted to know the measurements of the element of the circuit we wanted to show and which parts we wanted to remain hidden. Using calipers, we measured each element of our circuit board including the overall size of the board, the size and distance of the display parts, the size of the lights, the size of the buttons and etc. Essentially, we drew the entire circuit face and measured all the parts of it.

Alarm Clock CAD Design

Next, we transferred those drawings and measurements to the CAD design in Fusion 360 software. Using fusion, we constructed a box design that included all the measurements of the circuit board we constructed. We created openings in the light display and button portions. The most challenging part of this process was getting the precise measurements we desired and transferring that to the software. We needed everything to fit snug. We constructed two parts to the alarm clock. The first part was the face of the clock and the second part was the box container where the battery would be held. The two pieces would fit together.

Alarm Clock Hardware Assembly

The final part of the process was placing the two printed designs together. Once they were fully printed, we placed the circuit board in the top part of the alarm print. We alligned the openings with the extruded parts of the circuit board. We hotglued the circuit board. Next, we connected the battery to the circuit board and enclosed the two pieces together. Once that was done, our alarm clock was fully functional and done.

Final Result and Reflection

Our final alarm clock design was complete and functional. We set the time to the exact time in our location. Overall, this project was fun and innovative. The most difficult part of the process was the CAD design because Fusion 360 was a brand new software that we had to work with. If i had the opportunity, I would add buttons in the 3D print design to help with functionality of the alarm clock.
